The Clinical Question (PICO)

Population: Adult patients in UK EDs with sepsis (NEWS2 â‰¥ 5) requiring IV fluids within one hour.

Intervention: IV fluid resuscitation with 5% Human Albumin Solution (HAS) for the first 6 hours.

Comparison: IV fluid resuscitation with a balanced crystalloid (e.g., Hartmann's or Plasmalyte).

Outcome: Primary Feasibility Outcomes: Recruitment rate and 30-day mortality. Secondary Outcomes: Hospital/ICU length of stay, protocol adherence, fluid volumes, complications.

Study Design

  • Multicentre (15 UK NHS hospitals)
  • Open, parallel-group randomised feasibility trial
  • 1:1 randomisation, stratified by age, lactate, and site
  • Investigator-led
  • Recruitment period: 12 months (June 2021 - June 2022)

Clinical Bottom Line

This feasibility trial suggests a definitive, large-scale trial is possible. However, given the observed trend towards higher mortality and complications with 5% HAS in this ED sepsis population, and its higher cost, such a trial is unlikely to show a benefit for albumin over balanced crystalloids and may even suggest harm. Current practice of using crystalloids first remains appropriate.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Pragmatic design, reflecting real-world UK ED practice.
  • Multicentre nature enhances external validity (generalisability).
  • High protocol adherence for receiving the allocated fluid.
  • Successfully recruited a relevant, high-risk patient group.

Limitations

  • Feasibility trial: Not powered to detect differences in clinical outcomes like mortality.
  • Open-label design: Clinicians knew the treatment, creating potential for performance bias.
  • Significant crossover: 22% of the albumin group also received crystalloids, which could dilute the treatment effect.
  • Intervention only mandated for 6 hours.

Potential FRCEM OSCE Questions

Examiner: "This was an open-label trial. What does that mean, and how might it have affected the results?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Define Open-Label: "An open-label (or unblinded) design means that both the clinicians and the patients were aware of which treatment was being administered."
  2. Explain the Risk (Performance Bias): "This introduces a risk of performance bias. For example, a clinician might manage a patient they know is receiving albumin differently from one receiving crystalloid. They might have a lower threshold for ordering other interventions, like vasopressors or diuretics, based on their preconceived ideas about the fluid's properties."
  3. Link to a Specific Finding: "This could potentially explain some of the secondary findings, such as the higher rates of pulmonary oedema (14.8% vs 7.4%) and vasopressor use (12.2% vs 8.1%) in the albumin arm, although these differences were not statistically significant."

Examiner: "The authors conclude that a definitive superiority trial is unlikely to show a benefit for albumin. Do you agree with this conclusion, based on their findings?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Agree with the Conclusion: "Yes, I agree with the authors' conclusion."
  2. Justify Using Primary Outcome: "Although this was only a feasibility trial and not powered for clinical outcomes, the point estimate for 30-day mortality was higher in the albumin group. The adjusted odds ratio was 1.50, and while the confidence interval (0.84-2.83) crosses one, it is notable that the interval is wide and does not suggest a strong signal of benefit for albumin. In fact, it doesn't rule out significant harm."
  3. Incorporate Secondary Outcomes: "Furthermore, several secondary outcomes, such as the need for critical care interventions and rates of complications like pulmonary oedema, also trended in favour of the crystalloid arm. "
  4. Synthesise and Conclude: "Given that albumin is significantly more expensive and these findings show no signal of benefit and a potential signal for harm, it is reasonable to conclude that a large, expensive definitive trial is unlikely to be worthwhile or demonstrate a clinically important advantage for albumin in this population."

Examiner: "How does this study apply to your practice in the Emergency Department? Would you change your management of sepsis based on this paper?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Assess External Validity: "The study has good external validity for my practice. It was conducted in 15 UK NHS hospitals, and the patient populationâ€”adults with sepsis and a NEWS score of 5 or moreâ€”is exactly the cohort we manage daily in the ED."
  2. State Impact on Practice (or lack thereof): "I would not change my current practice based on this paper, but it does reinforce it. Current international and NICE guidelines already recommend crystalloids as the first-line fluid for sepsis resuscitation."
  3. Explain Why: "This paper, while only a feasibility study, provides no evidence to challenge that guidance. The findings suggest that using albumin as a primary resuscitation fluid offers no benefit and may even be associated with worse outcomes. Therefore, I will continue to use balanced crystalloids as my first-choice resuscitation fluid for patients with sepsis in the ED."
